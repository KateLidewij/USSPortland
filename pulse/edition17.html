Now, twelve years is a long time, but this ship is equipped with the latest technologies that will support its efforts such as the < REDACTED > and the < REDACTED >. Not to mention < REDACTED >.

Captain's Corner: Why I love the Miranda Class
Captain Alenis Meru

A couple months ago, I wrote a little article for the Pulse about ship classes. I thought I would follow that up here with a little explanation about why I chose the Miranda. It may have been an odd choice – a Miranda class in 2391 – but when I chose it, it was the only one of its kind in the fleet and the Portland had been amazingly successful so far.

First, there is no small number of reasons which at first glance might be superficial, but which I think add up to a little more personal connection to the ship. I came into Star Trek via the original series and the original six movies, so I have a certain appreciation for the older ships and how they connect the prime OF-verse to the Star Trek I have the most nostalgia for. Not to mention that The Wrath of Khan is one of my favourite movies, and I love the classic lines of the TOS/movie era ships. An archaic bucket of bolts like the Portland has a certain character, which in my opinion, is lacking in a lot of the newer ships.

Second, I had some idea what I wanted. I didn’t want a gigantic ship that could overpower anything in the quadrant, so the Elysium-class was out. My vision for Star Trek is one of a Starfleet which is primarily meant for peaceful exploration and humanitarian initiatives, and a very combat-oriented vessel like a Defiant or a Prometheus wouldn’t really fit in well with my vision and the kinds of missions I wanted to run. I wanted to do a variety of missions, so I didn’t want something that was too specialized such as a Nova or an Olympic class. As such, a light, multi-role cruiser, was ideal.

I also had a vision that the old girl would be used as a testbed for new and experimental technologies, such as Project Mongoose and the Dark Energy Reactor. Not only does this help keep the engineering crew involved, but it also gives us the opportunity to really indulge in the technobabble and try out some unique systems. As an old design, one which was so reliable that it has been kept in service for over a century, the Miranda class is an ideal testbed for new technologies.

Finally, I think more important than the strengths of a ship are it’s weaknesses, because the weaknesses are a source of drama. And as a hundred year old ship, the Portland has a lot. It’s not the biggest, or the fastest, or the most powerful ship. But when we’re outrun and outgunned, that’s when the creative juices really get flowing.

To close with a quote from Captain Jean-Luc Picard,

The first ship I ever served aboard as Captain was called the Stargazer… it was an overworked, underpowered vessel that was always on the verge of flying apart at the seams. In every measurable way, my Enterprise is a superior ship. And yet… there are times I miss that cramped little Bridge very much.
